## A (very brief) introduction to research data management

Röder, Juliane

Published 2025-06-11

Licensed cc-by-4.0



In this 30 min talk, I introduced the audience of the Biodiversity and Conservation Colloqium at the biology department of the University of Marburg to basic aspects of research data management.&nbsp;
The first part discusses motivations and solutions for improved research data management specific for ecological and biodiversity data. The second part gives a very brief overview of simple, hands-on improvements of research data management along the data life cycle. Please refer to older versions of this presentation or to forschungsdaten.info (DE) and RDMkit by ELIXIR (EN) for a more detailed overview and additional resources. The third part showcases some tools and services for research data management provided by NFDI4Biodiversity (discipline specific), by the Hessian Research Data Management Infrastructures (HeFDI) (generic) and by the eResearch Center of the Philipps University of Marburg (generic).
To get started with research data management, feel free to take a look at the available&nbsp;NFDI4Biodiversity training resources, e.g. a detailed documentation of our NFDI4Biodiversity &amp; iDiv Seasonal School 2024, a one-week program from planning to publishing with slides, videos, training data sets, code and Jupyter notebooks. HeFDI&nbsp;provides workshops and Open Educational Resources (OERs) on research data management, too. The Philipps University of Marburg further offers various courses on research data management for Bachelor and Master students (MarSkills) and for PhD students (MARA), as well as generic and discipline specific OER in Ilias and more (e.g. Environmental Informatics working group). Finally, the Uni Marburg project EnableD curates a list of self-paced online tutorials on digital literacy for students, lecturers, researchers and administrative and technical staff.

## A Data Management Plan (DMP) for my research? Do I need one?

Linares, Jimena

Published 2024-07-31

Licensed cc-by-4.0



The production of research data is booming, but how can it be managed most effectively? 
Good data management starts with planning, which is the first step in the research data life cycle. Planning can be done with a Data Management Plan (DMP). By setting up a DMP, you outline what data will be produced and how it will be handled, preserved, and shared. As a formal document, a DMP benefits researchers and local organizations ensuring FAIRness and policy compliance.
By joining this session you will receive a detailed introduction to the concept of Data Management Plans, a guided tour of the GFBio service for DMP support, and the opportunity to explore the DMP Tool on your own.

## A Dataset of Württemberg's 19th-century fauna

Teich, Maximilian C.

Published 2026-01-10

Licensed cc-by-4.0



The southern German kingdom of W&uuml;rttemberg was administratively divided into districts, called "Ober&auml;mter" in German. Between 1824 and 1886, the royal statistical office published one regional study on each of the districts, including text descriptions of the local fauna.
The CSV data represent all species documented in these sources, with the follwing information for each mention:


district: Name of the district in which the species was mentioned.


year: Year of publication of the source text.


original_text: Excerpt from the text in which the species is mentioned.


german_name: German name of the animal as given in the text. May be empty if a scientific name is provided.


scientific_name: Scientific name of the animal as given in the text. May be empty if a German name is provided.


reported_occurrence: Interpretation of whether the source text describes the species as occurring in the district. False indicates the species is explicitly reported as absent; True is assumed when False is not set. This column reflects the content of the historical text and does not imply biological presence of a species.


normalized_name: Scientific name of the species as used by GBIF.


GBIF_Id: Identifier assigned to the species by GBIF.

## Accepting Mortality: Strategies for Ultra-Long Running Stateful Workloads in K8s

Beyvers, Sebastian, Hansen, Maria

Published 2024-11-13

Licensed cc-by-4.0



"Pods are mortal" is a well-known quote in the official Kubernetes documentation. For ultra-long running stateful workloads that take months to complete, this mortality comes with its own challenges. How do you react to hardware failures? What resource quotas are appropriate? What if the workload has no built-in persistence and does all its work in memory? For such workloads, failures can be fatal, potentially wiping out months of work. This session will show that despite all the obstacles, Kubernetes can still be a reasonable choice for running stateful workloads that take months to complete. Using real-world examples based on production workflows, we will show how we design, configure, run, and operate such workloads using K8s and Argo workflows. We will also show how intelligent checkpointing using CRIU can help us deal with failures and enables us to avoid some problems even before they occur.&nbsp;

## Advancing FAIR Data Management in Livestock: Towards Standardized Metadata

Fischer, Sarah O., Holetschek, Jörg, Melzer, Nina

Published 2024-08-15

Licensed cc-by-4.0



The field of farm animals is embarking on a journey towards FAIR (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, and Reusable) data management, which is also relevant to the 3R (reduce, replace, refine) principle for animal testing. However, metadata standardisation remains a major challenge, as it is often overlooked due to the absence of a commonly accepted standard in the livestock community. As a starting point and regarding the species-specific projects PIGWEB and IGG, we focus mainly on phenotypic data. The FBN is also part of the NFDI4Biodiversity project. In this context,&nbsp; a comparative analysis of established and ratified standards was conducted. ABCD standard proved a promising candidate for establishing standardised metadata, as it is very flexible and allows the integration of missing terms. Moreover, the ABCD may also serve as a basis for other livestock data. This work highlights the imperative of adopting standardised metadata practices to enhance FAIR data in respect to accessibility, reusability and interoperability.

## Advancing marine research through integrated metadata management at IOW

Placke, Manja, Hassenrück, Christiane, Feistel, Susanne, Jürgensmann, Susanne

Published 2025-12-16

Licensed cc-by-4.0



Abstract:
The Leibniz Institute for Baltic Sea Research Warnem&uuml;nde (IOW) maintains one of the longest continuous data series of marine observations, which began in the 1950s. Over the decades, the integration of evolving technologies and methodologies has played a crucial role in expanding the institute&rsquo;s research capabilities. Since the turn of the millennium, the technological progress has accelerated exponentially, creating new opportunities for modern marine research. At the same time, climate change and other anthropogenic influences pose new challenges from which further research tasks arise. Addressing these challenges requires advances across several domains, including measurement technologies &amp; instrumentation, modeling, bioinformatics and research data management.
The IOW is committed to evolving its data management infrastructure in line with modern best practices, including the FAIR data principles and following community standards for data and metadata. One key element of this approach is IOW&rsquo;s new database framework "MetaTransfer". It is designed to connect metadata of the often heterogeneous data outputs from the various marine scientific disciplines by enriching the metadata with information about related datasets and the research environment. Moreover, it aims to simplify the metadata collection process for data creators. The poster presentation provides details on how this framework might be achieved.

## BIIGLE: Bild- und Videoannotation für die Biodiversitätsforschung

Martin Zurowietz, Jens Stoye, Tim W. Nattkemper

Published 2022-10-12

Licensed cc-by-4.0



Bild- und Videodaten sind eine wichtige Grundlage f&uuml;r die Biodiversit&auml;tsforschung. Diese Daten werden prim&auml;r mittels Bild- und Videoannotation ausgewertet, also dem Markieren und Beschreiben von Objekten in den Bildern und Videos. Die Auswertung gro&szlig;er Bild- und Videodatens&auml;tze erfordert sehr viel Zeit. Daher ist spezielle Software besonders wichtig, um bei dieser Aufgabe zu unterst&uuml;tzen.

BIIGLE ist eine web-basierte Software zur Bild- und Videoannotation. Sie erm&ouml;glicht kollaboratives Arbeiten auf gro&szlig;en Bild- und Videodatens&auml;tzen. Neben Werkzeugen zur manuellen und computergest&uuml;tzten Annotation bietet BIIGLE auch M&ouml;glichkeiten zur Qualit&auml;tskontrolle oder der Erstellung von eigenen Taxonomien zur Beschreibung von Objekten. BIIGLE wird von Forschenden aus der ganzen Welt akzeptiert und genutzt.

Auch wenn BIIGLE bereits viele Funktionen beinhaltet, gibt es noch ein gro&szlig;es Entwicklungspotenzial, auch im Hinblick auf eine Eingliederung in NFDI4Biodiversity. So k&ouml;nnen Workshops angeboten werden, um die Sichtbarkeit von BIIGLE in der Community zu erh&ouml;hen. Weiterhin kann BIIGLE durch die Unterst&uuml;tzung von Single-Sign-On und Core Storage in die Research Data Commons integriert werden.

BIIGLE ist Open Source Software. Eine &ouml;ffentliche BIIGLE-Instanz wird &uuml;ber die de.NBI Cloud an der Universit&auml;t Bielefeld betrieben und kann von Forschenden kostenlos genutzt werden.

## BioMe - The butterfly monitoring Germany usecase

Harpke, Alexander, Brünecke, Jörg, Bohring, Hannes, Grescho, Volker, Haase, Kristina, Kühn, Elisabeth, Kuhnert, Tobias, Musche, Martin, Petruschke, Sven, Schnicke, Thomas, Sielaff, Daniel, Strätling, Jeremy, Voigt, Michael, Bumberger, Jan

Published 2024-08-28

Licensed cc-by-4.0



Butterfly Monitoring Germany -&nbsp;Based on the modular BioMe - infrastructure for biodiversity monitoring projects
Butterfly Monitoring Germany (Tagfalter-Monitoring Deutschland / TMD) is a citizen science project to assess the status and trends of butterfly populations in Germany. The data collected is used to analyse the impacts of environmental change such as habitat loss and climate change, to support evidence-based conservation, and to inform decision makers and the general public about the state of biodiversity. Citizen science programs like TMD are also used in other fields, e.g. the monitoring of other insect groups, small watercourses and streams, or certain vertebrate species.&nbsp;
The TMD apps and BioMe&nbsp;
Programmes for biodiversity monitoring require complex infrastructures to collect, manage and prepare the data for various purposes. The TMD project is the first use case using the BioMe framework. The project aimes to provide a framework and infrastructure to implement applications for data collection and data curation in the context of biodiversity monitoring initiatives. This primarily addresses species or environmental status monitoring, but also data management, in particular the data quality workflow and data visualisation. For this purpose, BioMe supports data integration and synthesis for cross-thematic/interdisciplinary networking as a prerequisite for data products and further analyses. The role/rights-based data access and options for aggregated data preparation and its visualisation in diagrams or maps contribute to knowledge transfer and facilitate the involvement of strategic stakeholders.&nbsp;
Requirements for the technical platform&nbsp;

Focused on science - modular approach with reusable components for content-driven requirements of scientific communities within the scope of monitoring projects and field trails/data collection projects&nbsp;

A group of innovative facilitators has started to support development of overarching services for research data management (RDM) in Germany. As part of Base4NFDI, Service Stewards and Section Liaison Officers support the integration of a hitherto rather diverse service landscape.
Since March 2023, the National Research Data Infrastructure (NFDI), funded by the German Research Foundation (DFG), comprises the final 26 discipline-specific consortia. They cover a broad range of scientific disciplines, spanning all subject areas from the humanities and social sciences, to the engineering sciences and the life and natural sciences. The consortia are developing new or enhancing existing tools, services, and workflows to enable FAIR data sharing in their respective disciplines. They are also driving cultural change towards an open data culture. This will professionalize RDM in a very diverse landscape of technical and organizational solutions.
To support the development of missing links within an overarching NFDI portfolio, a joint initiative by all 26 NFDI consortia, Base4NFDI, supports the development of needed NFDI services, through a proposal-driven bottom-up process. These proposals are driven by the working groups within the NFDI Sections, where cross-cutting topics are addressed across the disciplinary boundaries of the consortia.
The Section Liaison Officers directly support the work of the Sections in matters directly related to preparing basic services development strategies, decisions and monitoring. Their central tasks include assistance with coordinating discussions on similar topics across Sections and monitoring needs for complementary activities and collaboration between working groups. But they also collect feedback from all stakeholders to further consolidate and continuously update the process model for basic service development based on NFDI and community requirements.

Section Liaison Officers and Service Stewards work hand-in-hand to support consortia and sections within NFDI to foster the process of a basic service landscape created by a diverse scientific community from bottom-up.

## Long-term Archiving in the NFDI

Markus, Katharina, Naumann, Kai, Schmalzl, Marcus, Watson, Juliane, Triebel, Dagmar

Published 2024-05-10

Licensed cc-by-4.0



Long-term archiving (LTA) aims to prevent data and information loss over the long term and intends to ensure sustainable access to information. Failure to correctly implement LTA can put data at risk.
Research data holdings in the German science system are considered vulnerable in this regard, leading to the declaration that LTA is an important task for the German National Research Data Infrastructure e. V. (Nationale Forschungsdateninfrastruktur, NFDI). Yet many aspects remain unclear, including the strategy and targets of LTA in various research disciplines, the status of implementation, and coverage of LTA services in NFDI. To clarify these points, NFDI Working Group Long Term Archiving, which forms part of the Section Common infrastructure has launched a survey in the NFDI consortia. The goal was to determine current status and requirements regarding LTA.
One of the survey&rsquo;s key findings indicated a need for a LTA consultation service, as many consortia and research organisations did not yet approach long-term preservation (i. e. LTA) of data. If they considered LTA at all, they usually did not target preservation beyond bitstream preservation. In general, knowledge gaps in various areas became clear. As a consequence, the consultation service should advise both consortia and institutions on long-term archiving of research data. Survey participants also called for dedicated LTA solutions which are tailored to individual research domains. Therefore, state-of-the-art solutions should be pursued in cooperation with the consortia, their disciplines, and the international LTA community. These solutions should also adopt jointly developed principles, regulations and standards. Other key requirements include improved long-term financial support of consortia and institutions with regard to LTA in accordance with FAIR principles and an increase in LTA-focused certification of existing research data services.

Task Area 4 - Data integration, exploration, and exploitation - the NFDI-Research Data Commons (NFDI-RDC, 4all &amp; 4future): This task area develops a Cloud-based research infrastructure that provides scientists, providers and users with the technical baseline for exchanging data and services in a collaborative research environment. Das Gutachten wurde beauftragt, um die Befugnisse der Betreiber zur Weitergabe der &uuml;ber Jahre gesammelten Daten an die Global Biodiversity Information Facility (GBIF) zu kl&auml;ren. ## Working Group Charter. RDM Helpdesk Network

Engel, Judith, Helling, Patrick, Herrenbrück, Robert, Lemaire, Marina, Mehrtens, Hela, Schmidt, Marcus, Stellmacher, Martha, Weimer, Lukas, Wiljes, Cord, Zinke, Wolf

Published 2024-11-04

Licensed cc-by-4.0



Support is an essential component of an efficient infrastructure for research data management (RDM). Helpdesks guide researchers through this complex landscape and provide reliable support about all questions regarding research data management, including support for technical services, best practices, requirements of funding organizations and legal topics. In NFDI, most consortia have already established or are planning to establish helpdesks to support their specific communities. On a local level, many institutions have set up RDM helpdesks that provide support for the researchers of their own institution. Additional RDM support services are offered by RDM federal state initiatives, by research data centers, by specialist libraries, by the EOSC, and by providers of RDM-relevant tools. Helpdesks cover a wide range of institutions, disciplines, topics, methodologies and target audiences. However, the individual helpdesks are not yet interconnected and therefore cannot complement one another in an efficient way: Given the wide and constantly increasing complexity of RDM, no single helpdesk can provide the expertise for all potential support requests. Therefore, we see great potential in combining the efforts and resources of the existing RDM helpdesks into an efficient and comprehensive national RDM support network in order to provide optimal and tailored RDM support to all researchers and research-related institutions in Germany and in an international context.

## Workshopbericht Trainingsdatensätze und FDM-Trainingsmaterial

Röder, Juliane, Scherreiks, Pascal, Dröst, Frederik, Dworczyk, Claudia, Gemeinholzer, Birgit, Reiners, Tobias Erik, Schwartz, Anna Lisa, Tschink, Daniel, Brand, Ortrun

Published 2024-06-12

Licensed cc-by-4.0



Wir ben&ouml;tigen dringend mehr und bessere Daten um im Angesicht der Biodiversit&auml;tskrise bessere Vorhersagen treffen und Handlungsoptionen erarbeiten zu k&ouml;nnen (IPBES 2019). Dabei werden bereits gro&szlig;e Summen in die Biodiversit&auml;tsforschung gesteckt &ndash; aber die so gewonnenen Daten sind bisher oft nur schlecht nachnutzbar. Eine Aufgabe von NFDI4Biodiversity ist es, das Management von Forschungsdaten zu verbessern, und so mehr und vielf&auml;ltigere Daten nutzbar zu machen.
Um den Umgang mit Forschungsdaten einzu&uuml;ben, ben&ouml;tigen wir u.a. auch Trainingsdatens&auml;tze, mit denen Forschungsdatenmanagement (FDM) einge&uuml;bt werden kann, und die als Vorlagen f&uuml;r die eigenen Daten dienen &ndash; sei es, um eigene Daten entsprechend eines bestimmten Metadatenstandards zu ver&ouml;ffentlichen, um bestimmte Softwaretools benutzen oder um die eigenen Daten m&ouml;glichst gut mit anderen Daten verkn&uuml;pfen zu k&ouml;nnen.
Im Bereich Biodiversit&auml;tsforschung, &Ouml;kologie und Umweltwissenschaften hat der Begriff &bdquo;Trainingsdatensatz&ldquo; verschiedene Bedeutungen: K&uuml;nstliche Intelligenz wird mit Trainingsdatens&auml;tzen auf Mustererkennung trainiert (z.B. f&uuml;r die automatische Identifikation von Pflanzen), Datens&auml;tze werden zur Sch&auml;tzung von Parametern f&uuml;r statistische und andere Modelle in Trainings- und Testdaten geteilt, in der Lehre werden Trainingsdatens&auml;tze zur Vermittlung von fachlichen Inhalten genutzt, und schlie&szlig;lich vermitteln Trainingsdatens&auml;tze Aspekte des Forschungsdatenmanagements. 
Forschungsdatenmanagement ist Teil der Guten Wissenschaftlichen Praxis (DFG Kodex 2022), deshalb werden in der Lehre meist fachliche und formelle Inhalte gleichzeitig vermittelt. Allerdings gibt es bisher z.B. mehr Trainingsdatens&auml;tze die bestimmte statistische Analysemethoden vermitteln als solche die dabei helfen das Ver&ouml;ffentlichen eigener Daten oder andere Aspekte des Forschungsdatenmanagements zu &uuml;ben.
Im Rahmen der NFDI4Biodiversity All Hands Conference im Mai 2024 in M&uuml;nchen organisierten wir deshalb einen Workshop zum Thema &bdquo;Trainingsdatens&auml;tze und FDM-Materialien&ldquo;. Dort sammelten wir gemeinsam Kriterien f&uuml;r gute Trainingsdatens&auml;tze, charakterisierten einige von uns bereits in FDM-Kontexten verwendeten Trainingsdatens&auml;tze entsprechend Struktur, Inhalt, Herkunft, und Grad der Aufbereitung, und ordneten diese Trainingsdatens&auml;tze Stationen des Datenlebenszyklus zu. Hier berichten wir die Ergebnisse dieses Workshops.
